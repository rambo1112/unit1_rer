Introduction to Non-Conventional Energy

Non-conventional energy resources, often called renewable energy, are sources that are naturally replenished on a human timescale. Unlike finite conventional sources like coal and petroleum, they offer a sustainable and environmentally cleaner alternative. The global shift towards these resources is driven by three main factors:

Environmental Concerns

Combustion of fossil fuels releases greenhouse gases (e.g., CO2), driving climate change. Renewables have a minimal carbon footprint.

Energy Security

Reduces dependence on foreign fossil fuel imports, enhancing national security and economic stability.

Sustainability

Fossil fuels are finite. Renewables provide a long-term energy supply for future generations.

Availability of Resources

The potential for renewable energy is immense, though it varies geographically. The sun provides more energy in one hour than humanity consumes in a year.

Indian Context

India has a rich and diverse renewable energy resource base. As of recent data, India's installed renewable capacity has crossed 180 GW.

  • Solar: Excellent potential with 4-7 kWh per sq. meter per day in most regions. A cornerstone of India's energy policy.
  • Wind: 4th largest installed capacity globally, with strong potential along coastlines and in states like Gujarat and Tamil Nadu.
  • Hydropower: Significant potential in the Himalayan region and other major river systems.
  • Biomass: High potential from agricultural residue, supporting rural economies.

Theory of Solar Cells

A solar cell (or photovoltaic cell) converts sunlight directly into electricity via the **photovoltaic effect**. This process relies on a semiconductor structure called a **P-N Junction** to create and separate electrical charges.

The P-N Junction: Heart of the Cell

A P-N junction creates a built-in electric field. It's formed by joining two types of doped silicon:

  • N-type Silicon: Doped with an element like Phosphorus, creating an excess of free electrons (negative charge carriers).
  • P-type Silicon: Doped with an element like Boron, creating an abundance of "holes" (positive charge carriers).

The Photovoltaic Effect: 3 Key Steps

1️⃣

Light Absorption

A photon with sufficient energy strikes an atom in the silicon, exciting an electron.

2️⃣

Charge Generation

The excited electron jumps to a free state, leaving a "hole" behind. This creates an electron-hole pair.

3️⃣

Charge Separation

The P-N junction's electric field sweeps the electron to the N-side and the hole to the P-side, creating a voltage.

Solar Cell Materials

1st Gen: Crystalline Silicon (c-Si)

Dominates the market. Based on silicon wafers.

  • Monocrystalline (mono-Si): Highest efficiency (18-24%). Uniform black look. Most expensive.
  • Polycrystalline (poly-Si): Lower efficiency (15-20%). Blue, marbled look. Cheaper to produce.

2nd Gen: Thin-Film

Use thin layers of material. Flexible and lightweight but less efficient.

  • Cadmium Telluride (CdTe): Most common thin-film. Cost-effective.
  • Copper Indium Gallium Selenide (CIGS): Good efficiency, complex manufacturing.

3rd Gen: Emerging Tech

Mostly in R&D. Focus on high efficiency and low cost.

  • Perovskites: Rapidly improving efficiency but poor stability.
  • Organic PV (OPV): Flexible and cheap, but low efficiency and short lifespan.

Solar Array & Power Plant

From Cell to Array: A Modular System

Solar power systems are built by combining smaller units into larger ones to generate usable amounts of power.

Solar Power Plant Components

A large-scale solar farm feeds electricity into the grid. Its key components include:

  • Solar PV Array: The core component that captures sunlight.
  • Mounting Structures: Can be fixed or use tracking systems to follow the sun.
  • Inverters: Convert the DC electricity from panels into grid-compatible AC electricity.
  • Transformers: Step up the voltage for efficient long-distance transmission.
  • Control System: Monitors performance and manages the plant's operation.

Limitations of Solar Cells

Despite its benefits, solar power faces several challenges that must be considered for widespread adoption.

Intermittency

Solar panels only generate power when the sun is shining. No power at night and reduced output on cloudy days.

Energy Storage

Requires expensive battery systems to provide a consistent 24/7 power supply, increasing overall project cost.

High Initial Cost

Although prices are falling, the upfront investment for panels, inverters, and installation remains significant.

Land Use

Large-scale solar farms require vast areas of land, which can compete with agriculture or other land uses.

Efficiency Limits

Commercial panel efficiency is typically 17-22%. Much of the sun's energy is lost as heat.

Manufacturing & Disposal

Panel production is energy-intensive and can involve hazardous materials. Recycling old panels is an emerging challenge.
